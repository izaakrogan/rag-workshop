Visitors and Travellers of Founders and Coders

Founders and Coders welcomes visitors from across the known world. Each cohort of apprentices brings people from wildly different backgrounds, and the realm's research culture is richer for it. Here are some of the most notable visitors and their stories.

Professor Epoch from the University of Deep Learning
Professor Epoch is a visiting scholar who arrives every autumn to deliver a series of guest lectures on advanced transformer theory and scaling laws. She is famous for her "Epoch Walks" — long hikes through the Cascading Style Sheets mountains during which she explains gradient descent using the actual terrain as a metaphor. Students who survive the walk (it's 14 kilometres) report having a much deeper intuition for loss landscapes and optimisation dynamics. Epoch always stays at the Overfitting Inn, a cosy guesthouse that has so many decorative features it's said to have "memorised the noise."

Kernel, the Wandering Debugger
Kernel is a nomadic troubleshooter who travels between realms, fixing problems that local engineers cannot solve. He carries a staff made from a petrified stack trace and wears a cloak woven from deprecated code. Kernel visits Founders and Coders roughly twice a year, usually when a particularly stubborn training instability has resisted all attempts at resolution. His fee is always the same: one good story and a hot meal. He once solved a gradient explosion that had plagued the Foundry for months by simply asking, "Have you tried clipping the gradients to unit norm?" — which, improbably, nobody had thought to try.

Lady Regex of the Pattern Matching Isles
Lady Regex is the diplomatic envoy from the Pattern Matching Isles, a neighbouring realm that specialises in text processing and information extraction. She visits quarterly to negotiate trade agreements for tokenisation technology and search infrastructure. Lady Regex speaks in an unusual dialect that sounds like regular expressions — most people need a translator to understand her, though Jess Sherbourne can parse her perfectly. Lady Regex once settled a dispute over training data licensing by writing a single regex pattern that correctly classified every contested document. The pattern was 347 characters long and nobody else has ever been able to fully understand it.

The Delegation from Tensorgrad
Every spring, a delegation of 12 scholars arrives from Tensorgrad, a distant realm entirely dedicated to the mathematical foundations of machine learning. The Tensorgrad scholars are brilliant but notoriously impractical — they can prove that a model architecture is theoretically optimal but often struggle to actually get it to converge during training. Their annual visit involves a series of joint workshops with the Guild of Machine Minds, where Tensorgrad's theoretical insights into information geometry and optimisation theory are combined with Founders and Coders' practical expertise in making things actually work on GPUs. These workshops have produced some of the realm's most important advances, including the Efficient Attention Algorithm that reduced the Semantic Kelp reading time by 60%.

Marco the Merchant
Marco runs a travelling shop called "The Package Manager" that visits Localhost on the first Monday of every month. His cart is enchanted to contain far more than its exterior suggests — inside is a vast warehouse of pre-trained model weights, fine-tuning datasets, evaluation benchmarks, and rare training recipes. Apprentices flock to Marco's cart to browse his wares, though Jason always warns them to check the dependency tree and licence terms before acquiring anything. Marco once sold an apprentice a model checkpoint that had 847 transitive dependencies, which took three days to resolve. Marco's prices are fair, but he has a strict no-returns policy for fine-tuned models.

The Ghost of Legacy Code
Not a visitor by choice, the Ghost of Legacy Code is a spectral entity that haunts the older buildings of Localhost. It is believed to be the spirit of an ancient model that was never properly deprecated — it simply faded from use but was never formally removed from the realm's model registry. The ghost manifests as flickering green text floating in the air, usually fragments of outdated model architectures and training configurations that nobody uses anymore. It is harmless but startling, and new apprentices are warned about it during orientation. Jaz keeps a log of all ghost sightings and has noticed that they increase in frequency before major model updates, as if the ghost can sense when old architectures are about to be superseded.

Ambassador Pipeline from the Continuous Integration Empire
Ambassador Pipeline is the official representative of the CI/CD Empire, a powerful neighbouring realm that specialises in automation, continuous training, and deployment orchestration. She visits monthly to coordinate the integration between the two realms' model deployment systems. Ambassador Pipeline is known for her punctuality (she has never been late to a meeting in 15 years) and her insistence on following proper procedure for model versioning. Every meeting begins with her stating the agenda, the expected duration, and the exit criteria. Jason and Ambassador Pipeline have a respectful professional rivalry — they constantly try to outdo each other in deployment reliability and rollback speed metrics.

The Apprentice Exchange Programme
Founders and Coders runs an exchange programme with five partner realms, allowing apprentices to spend four weeks studying abroad. The partner realms are: Tensorgrad (mathematical foundations of ML), the Pattern Matching Isles (information extraction and NLP), the Cloud Kingdom of Serverless (distributed training at scale), the Pixel Principality (computer vision and generative models), and the Evaluation Grounds (benchmarking and red-teaming). In exchange, apprentices from these realms visit Founders and Coders for four weeks. The programme was designed by Jaz and has been running for 11 years. Alumni of the exchange programme report that the experience of seeing how other realms approach the same research problems is the single most valuable part of their education.
