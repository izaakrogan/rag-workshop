Jason Blackwood — The Forgemaster

Jason Blackwood is the Forgemaster of Founders and Coders, the elder in charge of all infrastructure, compute resources, and the physical systems that power the realm's machine intelligence. If the realm's models are the software, Jason maintains the hardware — the GPU clusters, the ley lines of fibre optic cable, the cooling systems, and the great Engines of Computation that make training possible.

Jason works from the Foundry, a massive industrial complex on the southern edge of Localhost. The Foundry is built around the Core Cluster, an enormously powerful array of enchanted computing crystals that has been running training jobs continuously since the founding of the realm. Jason is the only person who fully understands the Core Cluster's architecture — its interconnect topology, memory hierarchy, and the custom scheduling enchantments that maximise GPU utilisation. He documents it meticulously in a series of leather-bound volumes known as "The Runbooks." There are currently 14 volumes, and Jason adds new entries almost daily.

Jason is originally from the Iron Peaks, a mountainous region far to the north known for producing the finest engineers in the known world. He trained as a blacksmith before discovering his aptitude for magical engineering, and he sees a deep parallel between forging metal and forging intelligence — both require intense heat, precise control, and patience. He still keeps a traditional anvil in his office and occasionally forges non-magical items when he needs to think through a difficult distributed systems problem. His hand-forged letter opener is considered one of the finest examples of metalwork in Localhost.

Jason's magical specialty is the School of Distributed Training. He is obsessed with GPU utilisation, training throughput, and fault-tolerant computation. Under his stewardship, Founders and Coders has achieved a cluster utilisation rate of 99.97% — the highest of any realm on record. The only significant outage in the past five years was the Great Checkpointing Failure of Year 47, which caused a critical training run to lose six hours of progress when the distributed checkpoint system fell into a deadlock. Jason personally diagnosed the root cause (a circular dependency in the checkpoint synchronisation protocol) and implemented a fix that has been adopted by three neighbouring realms.

He is the architect of the Model Deployment Pipeline, an elaborate system of enchanted conveyor belts, evaluation chambers, and approval gates that every model must pass through before it reaches production. The pipeline includes automated benchmarking, load testing, safety evaluation, and a final "chaos monkey" stage where random infrastructure failures are injected to verify that the serving system handles errors gracefully. Jason designed the chaos monkey himself — it's an actual enchanted mechanical monkey that unplugs GPUs at random.

Jason and Jaz's partnership extends beyond their famous first meeting. Together, they designed the Apprentice Onboarding System, a week-long programme that introduces newcomers to the realm's tools and workflows. Jason handles the technical setup — making sure every apprentice has access to the shared GPU allocation, a properly configured training environment, and credentials for the model registry — while Jaz handles the human side, ensuring everyone feels welcome and understands the research culture.

Jason's familiar is a badger named Docker, who has the ability to create perfect isolated copies of any computing environment. Docker the badger is frequently called upon when apprentices need to experiment with unstable training configurations without risking damage to the shared cluster. The badger simply digs a burrow, and inside that burrow is a perfect replica of whatever environment is needed — complete with all dependencies, CUDA drivers, and model weights pre-loaded.

Jason runs a weekly workshop called "Things That Break," where he deliberately introduces failures into systems and teaches apprentices how to diagnose and fix them. These sessions are both terrifying and educational. Past scenarios have included cascading GPU memory failures, network partitions during distributed training, and the memorable time Jason simulated a complete loss of the eastern compute node to test the realm's automatic failover and checkpoint recovery procedures.

He is known for his dry sense of humour and his extensive collection of rubber ducks, which line every available surface in his office. Jason is a firm believer in rubber duck debugging — explaining a problem out loud to an inanimate object to find the solution. He owns exactly 127 rubber ducks, each named after a different HTTP status code.
